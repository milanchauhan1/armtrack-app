# ArmTrack — College Essay Toolkit
*Scaffolding, not a ghostwritten essay. The final words must be YOURS — admissions readers (and AI detectors) can spot a voice that isn't a 17-year-old's, and an authentic version is genuinely stronger. Use this as the skeleton + raw material; you write it; I'll edit your drafts.*

## Your core narrative (the through-line)
**A player who got hurt because no one was tracking it → taught himself to build the tool he wished he'd had → made it free so the next kid doesn't learn the hard way.**

This works because it shows, without you having to claim them: initiative, technical skill, empathy, and integrity (you make $0). Don't *tell* the reader you're driven — the story proves it.

## The raw material (real, specific details = good essays)
Specificity is everything. Use these concrete beats:
- **The scene:** ibuprofen before practice, telling yourself you were fine, the elbow pain becoming "part of the routine." That's a vivid, honest opening image.
- **The turn:** it caught up with you — you still deal with it, and you think about how different it'd be if you'd paid attention sooner.
- **The build:** you taught yourself to ship a real app (Next.js, a database locked down for minors' data, the App Store). Name a *specific hard problem* you solved — e.g., realizing the invisible work (securing the data, designing a model you could *defend*) mattered more than the UI.
- **The decision that reveals character:** making it free, no ads, no selling data — "this isn't about money."
- **The data-science angle (for your major):** you didn't just build an app, you designed a *model* — and you were honest that a transparent heuristic beats a black box when lives/health are involved and there's no data yet. That intellectual honesty is the AI/data-science maturity signal.

## Common App personal statement — 3 angles to pick from
1. **"The invisible work"** (best for data-science fit): open on the pain, pivot to building — and the realization that the *unseen* engineering (security, the model) was the real work. Theme: depth over flash.
2. **"Free on purpose"**: center the decision to make it free and what that says about why you build. Theme: values / integrity.
3. **"Teaching myself"**: the self-directed journey from "I can't code this" to a shipped App Store app. Theme: agency / growth.

**Suggested structure (any angle):**
- Hook: a concrete, sensory moment (the ibuprofen / the pain).
- Stakes: why it mattered (yours, then bigger — the epidemic).
- Action: the building, with ONE specific hard problem you solved.
- Reflection: what it changed about how you think (this is where data-science maturity shows).
- Forward: who it's for now, and what kind of builder/student it made you.
